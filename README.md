# The Age of Average

> Average is not a number — it’s a decision.

## Overview

**The Age of Average** is an interactive data experience that challenges one of the most commonly accepted ideas in data: that “average” is fixed.

Instead, this project reframes average as **contextual, dynamic, and user-defined**.

Users enter their own profile, choose who they want to compare themselves to, and instantly see how their position shifts as the benchmark changes. What emerges is a powerful insight:

> You are not one level of “average” — you are many, depending on the group you choose.

---

## Core Concept

Most visualizations present average as a single line or static value.

This project asks a different question:

**What if the average itself moved?**

By allowing users to define their own comparison group (e.g., by gender, education, province, industry, or combinations of these), the visualization recalculates the benchmark in real time and shows where the user stands within that specific context.

---

## Key Features

### Dynamic Benchmarking
- Users define their own comparison group using one or multiple filters
- The population updates instantly based on selected attributes
- The meaning of “average” shifts with every interaction

### Living Population Visualization
- Each dot represents an individual from the dataset
- The distribution forms organically rather than as a static chart
- The user is highlighted as a distinct, glowing point

### Contextual Averages
- Average is shown as a **zone**, not just a line
- Users can visually understand where most people fall
- Immediate feedback: above average, below average, or near average

### Multi-Metric Exploration
- Compare across different dimensions
- Watch how your position changes depending on what you measure

### Narrative-Driven Interaction
- Designed as an experience, not just a tool
- Encourages exploration through changing perspectives
- Makes abstract statistical concepts intuitive and personal

---

## Why This Matters

“Average” is often treated as objective truth — but it’s not.

It depends on:
- Who you include
- Who you exclude
- What dimensions you prioritize

This project makes that invisible assumption visible.
